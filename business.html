Data Privacy and Security

Different entities such as virtual mobile network operators, communication service providers, and network infrastructure providers, will be crucial to the design, implementation, and upkeep of 5G networks as telecoms carry out the new wireless cellular technology globally. 5G mobile carriers are losing governance of security and privacy, in comparison to prior generations where mobile operators had direct access to and control over system components. Syncing mismatched rules will be a major difficulty in 5G networks due to the actors' differing preferences for security and privacy.

There were security problems previous generations of wireless cellular networks:

- Mobile phones and wireless channels were targeted for illicit cloning and impersonating through 1G networks in the 1980s.

- Message spamming became prevalent in the 2G networks, spreading unwanted marketing information.

- With the development of 3G networks and IP-based communication, security flaws discovered on desktop computers extended into the wireless networks.

- The expansion of smart gadgets and millions of third-party apps on 4G networks resulted in a dynamic and sophisticated threat landscape.

More than 7 trillion wireless gadgets servicing over 7 billion people will be networked by 5G wireless networks, introducing a new era of security challenges and a greater emphasis on privacy.

Privacy Challenges

Privacy concerns for users revolve around location tracking, identity, and other personal data. This is due to the signal is sent from a single cellular tower, 4G network technology has a wide coverage area. 5G networks have a much narrower coverage area than 4G networks, and the signal cannot penetrate barriers as well. As a result, 5G networks necessitate a large number of smaller antennas and base stations located both indoors and outside. The cell tower or antenna with which a mobile user communicates can provide important information about the user's location. When a user connects to a 5G antenna, mobile networks may pinpoint the user's location and even the building the user is in. Semantic information assaults (the use of inaccurate information to cause harm) frequently target users' location data. Access point selection algorithms in 5G mobile networks can also leak location data. Overall, additional 5G antennas enable exact location tracking of users both inside and outside the building. In terms of identity, International Mobile Subscriber Identity (IMSI) capturing attacks might reveal mobile subscribers' identities. An attacker intercepts mobile traffic in a limited area by grabbing the IMSI of the subscriber's device in order to track an individual's activity. An attacker can observe the number of outgoing calls or text messages exchanged, but not the contents of those messages. Even after a person has left the attack area, the attacker can continue track the quantity of previous and future calls or texts.

There are also risk factors of 5G technology in Cybersecurity companies:

1. Increase of Attack Surfaces

Hackers can exploit the attack surface of the network. Unlike 4G technology, 5G have more traffic and routing points. Multiple uncontrolled network entry points can provide hackers access to location tracking and potentially cellular reception for logged-in users. This new architecture also renders current cybersecurity practices obsolete, leaving the network vulnerable to deadly attacks.

IoT Security Standards

Many IoT devices are built with weak or non-existent cybersecurity standards. Hackers are already using these gadgets as entry points into company networks. It is possible for that such unsafe IoT gadgets could readily enable for man-in-the-middle assaults . A cybercriminal could intercept and alter the 5G traffic.

Dynamic Spectrum Sharing Makes Network Partitioning Complex

To limit cyber threats, current 4G systems include network partitioning techniques and to avoid the existence of a single point of failure, networks are split by hardware. If a network node is attacked, it can be "quarantined" to limit the attack without losing control of the entire network. In contrast, 5G uses short-range, low-cost, and small-cell physical antennas inside the geographic service area. Each antenna has the potential to become a single point of control. Botnet and denial of service (DDoS) attacks can bring down entire networks by just overloading a single node.
